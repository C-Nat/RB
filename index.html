Basis of Design: Rigid Debris-Resisting Barrier

Region: Hong Kong SAR | Standards: GEO Reports & Technical Guidance Notes (2025 Update)

1. Debris Mobility & Geometric Sizing

Ref: GEO Report No. 270 | GEO Report No. 339
  • Free-Field Extraction: Determine width of barrier and extract peak velocity (v_max) and flow thickness (h_max) from numerical models.
  • Retention Capacity: Calculated using the design landslide volume plus an 8% bulk factor. The storage wedge is modeled using an assumed storage angle (typically 1/2 to 2/3 of the natural slope).
  • Surge-up Height (h_s): Based on the Energy Principle: h_s = h + v²/(2g).
Justification: The 8% bulk factor accounts for volume dilation during transport. The surge-up check ensures the barrier crest level is sufficient to prevent overtopping from the frontal wave energy.

2. Dynamic Impact Force & α Coefficient

Ref: TGN 52 | GEO Report No. 358
  • Hydrodynamic Force: p_dyn = α ρ v²
  • Evolution of α: The coefficient is updated from 2.5 (GEO 270) to 1.5.
  • Load Classification: Reclassified as a Dead Load (Action) rather than a surcharge.
Justification: The reduction to 1.5 is based on local back-analysis of landslide events showing that 2.5 was overly conservative for rigid structures. Classification as a Dead Load reflects its nature as a primary gravity-driven mass.

3. Velocity-Stepped Filling Approach

Ref: GEO Report No. 339 | TGN 52
  • Legacy Approach (GEO 270): Applied peak thickness and peak velocity across all scenarios simultaneously.
  • Modern Approach (GEO 339): Introduced the Velocity-Stepped Assumption. The filling process is analyzed in stages.
  • Workflow: Debris arrives in surges of thickness h_max. The velocity for each stage v_n is correlated to the cumulative volume deposited. The impact is applied as a UDL over the current impacting thickness.
Justification: This recognizes that previously deposited debris provides a "cushioning effect" and that subsequent surges hit at a higher elevation on the stem with attenuated relative energy.

4. Geotechnical Stability (Geoguide 1)

Ref: Geoguide 1 | TGN 44
  • Partial Factors: Apply 1.2 on tan φ' (Soil Strength) and 1.0 on Earth Pressure.
  • Impacting Layer: Modeled as Drained.
  • Static Layer: Previous surges modeled as Undrained (Static) with K_a = 1.0.
  • Phreatic Surface: A water table is adopted at the interface of the two layers.
Justification: Adopting K_a = 1.0 for the static layer is a conservative requirement to account for the semi-fluid and unconsolidated state of freshly trapped debris.

5. Tie-Back Design & Global Stability

Ref: LPMit Design Tips 12 | GEO Paper 3/2013
  • Tie-Backs: Ultimate pull-out R_ult = 0.35 MPa × πDL_fixed. A Resistance Efficiency Factor (η) is applied to account for progressive debonding along the fixed length.
  • Global Stability: Hillside FOS must be 1.2 (CTL 1), 1.1 (CTL 2), or 1.0 (CTL 3) with the barrier and debris load in place.

6. Structural Sectional Design (ULS)

Ref: HK Concrete Code | GEO Report No. 339
  • Analysis: Cantilever model fixed at the intersection of centerlines.
  • Partial Factors: 1.4 for Adverse Loads and 1.0 for Beneficial Loads.
  • Uplift: Ignored for reinforcement sizing to ensure maximum robustness.
  • Scenario Separation: Peak flow impact and Boulder impact are analyzed as independent scenarios; they are not superimposed.

Basis of Design: LS-DYNA Coupled Analysis

Methodology: 3D finite element with barrier modelled

1. Direct Force & Surge Extraction

Ref: GEO Report No. 325 | GEO Report No. 358
  • Impact Extraction: The Impact Force vs. Time history is extracted directly from the coupled debris-structure model.
  • Surge-up Observation: Surge behavior and run-up heights are observed directly from simulation outputs, bypassing energy principle formulas.
Simplification Logic: Only one critical design scenario is assessed (Maximum Force). This force encapsulates both dynamic peak and static dominant phases, ensuring the most conservative structural envelope is used.

2. Forces on wall assumptions

  • Force Distribution: The maximum extracted force is applied as a UDL across the maximum pile-up thickness recorded in the simulation.
  • Pore Pressure: Water is intrinsic to the LS-DYNA force extraction; therefore, no additional lateral pore water pressure is superimposed.
  • Uplift Assumption: Uplift pressure is calculated from the top of the debris pile down to the base slab.

3. Stability Results & Comparative Justification

Comparison between LS-DYNA results and conventional 2D/Free-field methods:

Stability Check Observed Result Engineering Reasoning
Pile-up Thickness Smaller (↓) Coupled models account for 3D energy dissipation and lateral spreading.
FOS Sliding Greater (↑) Reduced pile-up thickness results in a lower total horizontal sliding force.
FOS Bearing Greater (↑) Reduced total debris mass and improved eccentricity due to realistic deposition profile.
FOS Overturning Greater (↓) Despite higher uplift (calculated from pile-up surface), the significantly reduced thickness results in a smaller destabilizing moment arm.

Methodology Mapping: Replaced vs. Retained Steps

1. Steps REPLACED by LS-DYNA Simulation

Conventional Step LS-DYNA Replacement
Multiple Scenarios with manual velocity-stepped graph Pile-up Simulation: The model clearly shows if debris arrives in surges or piles up slowly, making manual stage assumptions redundant.
Energy Principle Surge-up (h_s) Direct Simulation Observation: True splash-up and run-up are observed and measured directly from the model.
Dynamic force calculation from velocity correlation Direct Force Extraction: Real-time contact pressure is extracted from the interaction, replacing empirical hydrodynamic formulas.

2. Steps RETAINED (Must be done MANUALLY)

A. Geotechnical Stability Calculation

Verification of Sliding, Overturning, and Bearing Capacity (Geoguide 1) must still be done manually. The LS-DYNA force serves as the Action input for these stability equations.

B. Retention Capacity Verification

Manual proof of storage volume against V_design (including 8% bulk factor) is still required to justify barrier sizing.

C. Structural RC Reinforcement Design

Final rebar sizing, detailing, and crack control for the stem and base must follow the HK Concrete Code based on the extracted loads.

3. Conclusion/Evaluation

Benefits (Pros) Constraints (Cons)
More Realistic Interaction: Captures true structure-debris coupled behavior. Manual Retention: Does not replace the need for manual stability and capacity calculations.
Optimized Design: Often reduces stem requirements due to 3D dissipation. Computational Cost: Extremely long run-times and HPC requirements.

Design Guideline Evolution & References

Timeline: Changes following each GEO Report Update (2012-2023)

GEO Report No. 270 (2012)

Ref: GEO Report No. 270
  • Used the same design impact velocity for all phases of impact.
  • Ignored the beneficial self-weight of the debris (H_Deb) during stability assessment.

GEO Report No. 333 (2018)

Ref: GEO Report No. 333
  • Introduced a design velocity as a step function instead of a constant value (acknowledging velocity reduction upon impact).
  • Maximum design debris velocity is different for each phase (limited to 70% of max free-field design velocity).
  • Design debris impact thickness should be the larger of (i) maximum free-field thickness, (ii) a nominal thickness of 0.5m, or (iii) estimated thickness of accumulated boulders.

GEO Report No. 339 (2018)

Ref: GEO Report No. 339
  • The self-weight of impacting debris should be taken as **Dead Load** in design (not surcharge), allowing beneficial effects to be considered in stability analysis.
  • Superposition of debris flow impact load and boulder impact load is not required if the largest entrainable boulder is less than 0.5m.
  • Provision of baffles may optimize structural design by making superposition unnecessary.
  • Updates reduced barrier mass by approx. 60% compared to GEO 270 guidelines.

TGN 44, 47, 52 (2020-2023 Updates)

Ref: TGN 44 | TGN 47 | TGN 52
  • TGN 44 provides guidance on assessing debris flow velocity based on GEO Report 333 methods.
  • TGN 52 stipulated the use of α = 1.5 for impact calculation, based on further research and large-scale testing.
